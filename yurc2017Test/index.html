Organizing Committee

This conference was organized by Juli Coraor, Suryabrata Dutta, Nishant Jain, Daniel Lee, and Jingjing Xiao, who are all members of the founding board of the Yale Undergraduate Research Association (YURA). Visit our website to learn more at yura.yale.edu.

About YURS 2015

The Yale Undergraduate Research Symposium (YURS) is more than just a research conference. It is a discourse between and among the undergraduates of Yale and the rest of the Yale community, encouraging students to pursue their passions for research and facilitate the communication of ideas across all disciplines.

The Yale Undergraduate Research Association (YURA), as one of our founding missions, believes that a strong research community is paramount to enable the undergraduates at Yale to positively influence the world of research, both in the present and in their future careers. The skills and passions they learn now in the lab and in their studies will translate to the rest of their lives as they leave Yale and continue onto graduate school, medical school, industry, and beyond, culminating in the pursuit of their own research interests independently as principal investigators. It is our hope that by fostering such a strong research community among the Yale undergraduates, YURA will promote the inspiration and connections necessary to strengthen these ties to research, and ultimately influence students to continue in research and contribute to the pursuit of knowledge.

YURS furthers this goal by giving a voice to the undergraduates who conduct research at Yale. We promote the growth of our community uniquely through interdisciplinary communication. Our symposium exposes students to all methods of problem solving each discipline has to offer, from anthropology to political science to mathematics. Yale prides itself on its liberal arts, and YURS highlights this strength by illustrating that sharing concepts between the sciences, humanities, and social sciences fosters more impactful communication of ideas than connections solely within one discipline, broadening the minds of each researcher and inspiring creativity and innovation.

Through a day-long conference, students are able to present their research through posters and presentations. Their participation in inspiring each otherâ€™s research through questioning and active discussions will be critical to the advancement of our community, sparking conversations to give rise to new ideas - ideas which may in turn inspire the research of others. As we grow, we will empower our abilities, and our identity as pursuers of knowledge and truth. Together, as part of this conference and as a community, our possibilities are limitless.

Keynote Speakers

Jonathan Holloway, Dean of Yale College

Jonathan Holloway (GRD, 1995), is Dean of Yale College and Edmund S. Morgan Professor of African American Studies, History, and American Studies. He specializes in post-emancipation United States history with a focus on social and intellectual history.

Brian Scholl, Professor of Psychology

Brian Scholl is a professor in the Department of Psychology at Yale University, and the director of the Yale Perception and Cognition Laboratory. His research encompasses all aspects of perception, cognition, and how they relate to each other.

Jonathan Ellman, Eugene Higgins Professor of Chemistry

Jonathan Ellman is a Eugene Higgins Professor of Chemistry, Professor of Pharmacology, and Director of the Division of Physical Sciences & Engineering at Yale. His laboratory seeks to contribute to the incredible opportunities provided by chemical synthesis through the design and development of efficient and general new methods for achieving essential bond connections.

Joan Steitz, Sterling Professor of Molecular Biophysics and Biochemistry

Joan Steitz is a Sterling Professor of Molecular Biophysics and Biochemistry at Yale, and an investigator at the Howard Hughes Medical Institute. Once the advisee of James D. Watson, co-discoverer of the DNA double helix, Steitz is best known for her pioneering work in RNA. Her breakthroughs into the previously mysterious splicing process have clarified the science behind the formation of proteins and other biological processes.
